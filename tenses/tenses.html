The Twelve Tenses

It is important to use a range of tenses during your speaking and writing tests to show the examiner that you have a good and flexible knowledge of grammar. I have included the main uses of the tenses below, there are other less frequent uses of the tenses but I have excluded them, as the ones below will be sufficient up to band 7.5.

It is essential to understand what a time period is. A time period is a length of time, there are three main time periods, present, past and future. Each of these is sub-divided into two time periods, a simple time period and a perfect time period. Now here is the important thing to remember: they are not tenses, they are time periods. Time periods are associated with time; tenses tell us which time period a verb is being associated with - remember this difference, it is very important. It is these six time periods which we must understand because each of them has two verb tenses associated with it: a simple and a continuous.

The tenses therefore look like this:

Past Time Period


Past Simple Time Period

to which is associated:

Past simple tense
Past continuous tense


Past Perfect Time Period

to which is associated:

Past perfect tense
Past perfect continuous tense

Present Time Period


Present Simple Time Period

to which is associated:

Present simple tense
Present continuous tense


Present Perfect Time Period

to which is associated:

Present perfect tense
Present perfect continuous tense

Future Time Period


Future Simple Time Period

to which is associated:

Future simple tense
Future continuous tense


Future Perfect Time Period

to which is associated:

Future perfect tense
Future perfect continuous tense
