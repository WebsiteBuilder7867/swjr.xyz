The American desert produces, when conditions are favorable, gorgeous exhibition of wildflowers. Trees, shrubs, and herbs all contribute to splendour of the display. Soil composition, slope and exposure, suitable temperatures, and adequate moisture are essential to plant growth and flower production.

Moisture is uncertain factor, and years may pass without enough rainfall to stimulate plant growth. Rain of less than 0.15 inch is wasted as far as desert plants are concerned, for moisture evaporates before penetrating soil. Some annuals produce seeds having water-soluble germination inhibitors in their coverings, hence fail to sprout, even after rain, unless moisture totals at least half an inch.

When soil moisture from December and January rainfall is enough to support potential plants it dissolves seed coats, and desert floor is soon carpeted with eager green seedlings. When winter rains are scant, as is so often case, dormant seed population fails to germinate and spring flower display doesn’t appear. There is no sure way to forecast sudden cold wave or period of drying winds, which may prevent potential season of brilliant bloom. great flower year may occur only once in a decade.

Perennials are more dependable than annuals, since some of them, particularly cactuses and other succulents, have water storage tissues in their stems or roots. These perennials may be counted on to blossom each year. Many perennials have surprisingly extensive root systems. The ways by which plants manage to thrive under severe conditions of desert heat and drought are fascinating. As we have seen, most annuals lie dormant as seeds until suitable moisture and temperature occur. Then they grow very rapidly and bloom while soil still has sufficient moisture.

Another group of plants slows down life processes to become dormant during dry periods, even to dropping all leaves. When rains come they put on new leaves, several times year if necessary.

Cactuses and other succulents gorge themselves with water when soil is wet, releasing moisture very sparingly from storage tissues during “long dry.” Some have discarded or reduced foliage, or have covered leaf surfaces with varnish or wax, to decrease loss of vital moisture through transpiration to a minimum.

Afghanistan's economic outlook has improved significantly since fall of Taliban regime in 2001 because of infusion of over eight billion dollars in international assistance, recovery of agricultural sector and growth of service sector, and reestablishment of market institutions. Real GDP growth is estimated to have slowed in last fiscal year primarily because adverse weather conditions cut agricultural production, but is expected to rebound over 2005-06 because of foreign donor reconstruction and service sector growth. Despite progress of past few years, Afghanistan remains extremely poor, landlocked, and highly dependent on foreign aid, farming, and trade with neighboring countries. It will probably take remainder of decade and continuing donor aid and attention to significantly raise Afghanistan's living standards from its current status, among lowest in world. Much of population continues to suffer from shortages of housing, clean water, electricity, medical care, and jobs. However, officers and men of all Afghan government and international donors remain committed to improving access to these basic necessities by prioritising infrastructure development, education, housing development, jobs programs, and economic reform over next year. Growing political stability and continued international commitment to Afghan reconstruction is creating optimistic outlook for continuing improvements in Afghan economy in 2006. Expanding poppy cultivation and growing opium trade may account for one-third of GDP and looms as one of Kabul's most serious policy challenges. Other long-term challenges include: boosting supply of skilled labor, reducing vulnerability to severe natural disasters, expanding health services, and rebuilding a war torn infrastructure.
