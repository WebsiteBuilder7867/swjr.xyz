Idioms

The correct use of an idiom will be looked upon very favorably by your examiner, as it is a sign of an advanced speaker. It is unlikely that you will receive a band score of 7 and above without using idioms and phrasal verbs appropriately. Always use an idiom in an informal letter and in your spoken test. Do not use them in formal essays and graph descriptions up to band 7.5. At a very advanced level, IELTS band 8 and 9, I would advise their use in formal essays, but not all idioms are suitable. Choosing the correct ones and in the correct context is a native speaker level skill. Do not overuse them, two throughout the whole speaking test and one in an informal letter is enough. Using more than this may sound unnatural and forced.

Native British speakers use a lot of idiomatic speech. I have chosen the following selection because they are in contemporary use and are easy to insert into speaking and/or writing tests. For most tests, use just one or two, any more may be seen as excessive and unnatural.

Remember that you may hear an idiom in the listening test, so its important to understand what they all mean!

To start the ball rolling - to begin - to start the ball rolling, I would like to say that ...(ideal to start Part 2 of the speaking test!)

A golden opportunity - a good opportunity - I am trying my IELTS exam today, it is a golden opportunity to prove my level of English is good enough for promotion.

Cost me an arm and a leg - to emphasize that something is very expensive - I would like to visit America, but it would cost me an arm and a leg.

I will be waiting until the cows come home - to emphasize a long period of time, usually before a promise is fulfilled - My boss has promised me a raise (increase in salary), but I will be waiting until the cows come home.

Pigs will fly - to emphasize that something is extremely unlikely - I hope to win the lottery next year - pigs will fly! Only use this idiom at the end of a sentence.

The grass is greener - to emphasize that people want to emigrate or change their job because they think things will be different if they do - Many people want to emigrate to Canada because they think the grass is greener. People often change their jobs because they think the grass is greener.

My flesh and blood - a member of the family - I like my cousin a lot, she is my flesh and blood.

Face the music - accept your punishment or ordeal - I was late for work yesterday and had to face the music with my boss.

Bone of contention - a controversial subject - This subject is a bone of contention at the moment. (eg. Brexit, Donald Trump). Always use this in the passive, ie. something is a bone of contention. A bone of contention is usually between two people, or groups of people who have strong opposing views.

By the skin of my teeth - just succeeded - The last time I tried my IELTS, I got a 6.5 by the skin of my teeth.

To have a sweet tooth - to enjoy food that has a lot of sugar in it - I am not very good at dieting because I have a sweet tooth.

To be on cloud nine - to be very happy about something - When my boyfriend asked me to marry him, I was on cloud nine.

To keep your head above water - to stay out of debt - I need a good job to keep my head above water.

It was a piece of cake - very easy - I learned to ride a bicycle when I was a child, it was a piece of cake. Only use this idiom at the end of a sentence.

Can't make heads or tails of it - don't understand it - English grammar is very difficult, I can't make heads or tails of it.

Give a hand - give help to someone - My husband does all the gardening, but sometimes I give him a hand.

Come rain or shine - to overcome all obstacles - I am going to emigrate to Canada, come rain or shine.

Keep an eye on - to watch carefully - I sometimes keep an eye on my sister's children.

I am all ears - I am listening - Examiner: "I am going to ask you some questions." Student: "I am all ears." Use this only when someone says that he is going to tell you something you think is important.

She has him under her thumb - she has control of him - My daughter has her husband under her thumb.

She wears the trousers in the house - she is in charge in the house - In most western families, the woman wears the trousers (UK)/pants(US) in the house. You can also say "He wears the trousers/pands in the house."

She took to it like a duck in water - She learned how to do it with ease - My wife took to teaching like a duck in water.

On the ball - paying full attention - That security guard is always on the ball.

Sing his praises - to speak very well about someone else - my daughter keeps singing her boyfriend's praises. This idiom is usually used in the third person singular.

It's a pain in the neck - it is a nuisance - My washing machine keeps breaking down, it's a pain in the neck. Always state why something is a pain in the neck.

It was just a flash in the pan - an activity or interest that someone has for a very short time - He started to learn English but it was just a flash in the pan.

Right up my street - a job that you know and are comfortable with - Teaching on italki is right up my street.

To talk shop - to talk about work with your work colleagues - We all went to the pub after work and talked shop.

Prices have gone sky high - the cost of items in the shop has become very expensive - Prices have gone sky high since last year.

Strike while the iron is hot - to do it while you have the chance - It is snowing tomorrow and I want to go skiing, I will strike while the iron is hot. Always state why you are striking while the iron is hot.

Without rhyme or reason - for no reason - He was supposed to stay with us all week, but he left on Tuesday without rhyme or reason.

Take a leaf out of his book - to follow someone else's example - He did well in his exams because he studied every day, I think I will take a leaf out of his book.

Have something hanging over you - to be worried about something. - Your friend says: "The tax inspector wants to see my books, and they are in a mess." You answer: "you won't enjoy your holiday with that hanging over you."

Talk about the pot calling the kettle black - a hypocrite - Look at him telling that woman that she talks too loud (the man himself talks very loud) - Only use this idiom at the end of a sentence.

Fight to the bitter end - until you cannot do it any more - They want to build a factory next to my house - I will fight them to the bitter end.

It doesn't hold water - the theory cannot be upheld - Scientists say that there is life on Mars, but their theory doesn't hold water.

It is a matter of life and death - it is very serious and urgent - We need to get an ambulance quickly, it's a matter of life and death.

It was in one ear and out the other - to be completely ignored - I explained to the tax office that I could not pay them, but it was in one ear and out the other. Only use this idiom at the end of a sentence.

Save your breath - don't give me excuses - Employee: "I could not come to work today because my car broke down." Boss: "Save your breath."

Walk her up the aisle - to marry her - I am going to walk her up the aisle.

Water under the bridge - things that happened in the past, but are no longer relevant, usually referring to relationships or friendships - We had some good times together, but it is all water under the bridge now.

Paddle your own canoe - to do something yourself - I don't need any help, I can paddle my own canoe.

A slap on the wrist - a minor punishment - The policeman caught me and I got a slap on the wrist (let off with a warning).

Go with the flow - do as everyone else does - When you are in the Army, it is best to go with the flow.

You are spot-on - you are absolutely right - "I believe the new Prime Minister will change nothing." Reply: "Yes, you are spot-on." Sometimes we say,"you are spot-on there!"

Don't cry over spilled milk - not to be discouraged about a failure - I only got a six in my IELTS, but I need a seven, I won't cry over spilled milk.

Make a mountain out of a molehill - make a big issue out of a little one - My boss likes to make a mountain out of a molehill.

The world is your oyster - lots of opportunity - If you get an 8 in your IELTS test, the world is your oyster.

It is up in the air - temporarily disorganised, maybe during a transition - We are moving house next week so everything is up in the air at the moment.

Bring home the bacon - earn most of the income for the family - My wife looks after the house, I bring home the bacon.

Make hay while the sun shines - to make hay is to cut the grass so that it can be fed to horses and cows in winter, it means to take full advantage of an opportunity - I had a week off work, so I decided to make hay while the sun shines and study idioms.

With flying colours - to emphasize that something was very easy, usually associated with a good exam result - I passed my IELTS with flying colours.

To be let off the hook - to emphasize that someone was not punished, or did not have to pay money he owed - He was caught speeding, but the policeman let him off the hook. - He owed me money, but I let him off the hook.

I keep myself to myself - to emphasize that you do not interfere in other people's affairs - My work colleagues talk about the boss a lot, but I prefer to keep myself to myself.

Big deal - used sarcastically to imply triviality - Employee: "I got promoted today." Disinterested and maybe jealous friend: "Big deal."

It is not a big deal - used to imply that it is not very important - "It's only a clerical error, it's not a big deal."

Don't take it as Gospel - don't accept it as truth - Employee: "The boss told me that the company is financially sound (financially stable)." - Other employee: "Don't take it as Gospel."

Take it with a pinch of salt - it is probably untrue - My friend says he is going to work in Buckingham Palace, but I am taking it with a pinch of salt.

As safe as houses - safe - I have invested my money in Government Bonds, it is as safe as houses there.

As sound as a pound - safe and reliable - This investment is as sound as a pound. (This idiom may no longer be in use after Brexit LOL!)

Rome wasn't built in a day - it takes time to accomplish something - An opposition politician criticised Prime Minister Thatcher for introducing economic changes too fast, he said: "Rome wasn't built in a day." She replied: "Yes, but we weren't on that job!"

By hook or by crook - by any means possible - I will get that job by hook or by crook.

I need the job done by yesterday - urgent job - "I need it done by yesterday."

Keep in the dark - to not disclose information - The North Korean government likes to keep its citizens in the dark.

A week next Tuesday - next Tuesday is the Tuesday after the coming Tuesday, a week next Tuesday is the Tuesday after that, it is a sarcastic way of saying, "in the distant future." - Customer on the phone to the plumber who did not come to repair his tap: "Oh, so when are you going to do it, a week next Tuesday?"

On the tip of my tongue - trying to remember a word which is familiar - I remember him, but I can't remember his name, it's on the tip of my tongue.

Skeleton in the cupboard - something bad from your past that you don't want other's to know - Many politicians have skeletons in their cupboards.

Put the cart before the horse - to do things in the wrong order - Traditionally, milk goes in the cup before the tea is poured. However, many people put the cart before the horse now.

To feel blue - to feel a bit depressed or jaded - My boyfriend left me yesterday, so I am feeling a bit blue.

Put your foot down - to enforce the rules, to drive faster - My children were misbehaving yesterday, so I put my foot down. I was late for work, so I put my foot down on the motorway.

The future does not look rosy - the future looks negative - The future of the European Union does not look rosy.

Apple of my eye - a child that you adore, usually a son or daughter - My daughter is the apple of my eye.

To be on the safe side - to choose the safer option - I cannot decide whether or not to go for a walk because it looks like it is going to rain. I had better stay home, just to be on the safe side.

It's not my cup of tea - it is an activity I don't like - I don't like parachuting, it's not my cup of tea.

To look through rose-coloured (or tinted) glasses - to see something as very positive, even though it may not be - Don't look through rose-coloured glasses, your new job will be very difficult.

Caught red-handed - caught in the act of committing a crime - He has been arrested for stealing from the shop, he was caught red-handed.

There is a lot of red tape - a lot of paperwork - There is a lot of red tape when you open a business.

To be given the go-ahead or to be given the green light - to receive permission to do something - My manager gave me the green light to begin construction.

Throw in the towel - to give up in a competition or task - Obtaining a good grade in the IELTS is difficult, but I am not ready to throw in the towel yet.

A white lie - a harmless lie - I told my manager the job was overdue because of a late delivery, but it was a white lie, it was really because we all went to the pub.

Two wrongs don't make a right - revenge does not make things better - I felt like punching him in the nose for breaking my window, but two wrongs don't make a right.

I have been rushed off my feet - I have been very busy - I have been rushed off my feet all morning.

To butter someone up - make someone feel good before you ask them for something - I always butter my husband up before I ask if we can go on holiday.

To break a red light - drive through a red traffic light - I got fined for breaking a red light.

To brush up on something - to revise something that you have not studied for a long time - I need to brush up on my maths.

To start the ball rolling - to begin - to start the ball rolling, I will talk about my holidays in Spain.

Between a rock and a hard place - no solution to the problem - If I lend him my car, he will crash it, if I don't he will be offended - I am between a rock and a hard place.

A hard nut to crack - a difficult problem to solve - This maths question is a hard nut to crack.

Sweep it under the carpet - hide the mistake / crime - he was caught taking bribes, but it was all swept under the carpet.

A bright spark - a intelligent person - my son can do algebra at the age of 8 - he is a bright spark.

It's crystal clear - the situation is easy to understand - If the UK leaves the EU, other countries will do the same, it's crystal clear.

Sleep like a baby - to sleep well - I slept like a baby last night.

Call it a day - to finish a task, temporarily or permanently - we had worked hard and decided to call it a day.

Birds of a feather flock together - people of the same type associate with each other - "They all look like hippies!" "Yes birds of a feather flock together."

To look a gift-horse in the mouth - to examine something that was given to you to see if it is of good quality - "My father gave me his old bicycle. It in a very poor condition, but I should not look a gift-horse in the mouth."

To butter someone up - to praise someone with the intent of manipulating him - I always butter my boss up before I ask him for a day off.

In for a penny, in for a pound - to take a big risk rather than a small one - I think I will climb Mt Everest instead of Mt Snowdon (mountain in Wales). In for a penny, in for a pound.

To live on a shoestring - to live with a very low income. - I lost my job two months ago, I am living on a shoestring.

To live from hand to mouth - to have just enough money to live on. - Many people in Africa live from hand to mouth.

He who pays the piper calls the tune - people will do what the person who pays them asks

On your last legs - only just working. - My washing machine is on its last legs.

To let your hair down - to go out to a night-club or a party. It can sometimes mean just to relax. (Used by ladies only) - I have had a hard week at work, I'm going to let my hair down tonight.

To pop the question - to ask a woman to marry you.- He popped the question over lunch.

To tie the knot - to get married (UK English).- We decided to tie the knot.

To get hitched - to get married (US English). We decided to get hitched.

They are like chalk and cheese - the two people have very different personalities. - The two brothers were like chalk and cheese.

To eat someone out of house and home - to use up all of someone's financial resources - My son keeps wanting money to spend while in university, he is going to eat me out of house and home.

To have someone eating out of your hand - to have full control of someone - I have my husband eating out of my hand, he will do whatever I say.

Not all there - mentally lacking - He is not very good academically or socially, to be honest, I don't think he is all there.

To spend a penny - to use the toilet - I have drunk too much tea, I need to spend a penny.

To stick around - stay in the vicinity, or with us. - Stick around, we have nearly finished work, then we are going down the pub.

The watered down version - to have a weak version of something - The government wants a watered down version of Brexit, we will not be in the EU, but will still have free trade with no borders and the same laws as the EU has.

As sober as a judge - not drunk - the driver was tested for alcohol consumption, but he was as sober as a judge.

At death's door - almost dead - I went to see him in hospital, he is at death's door.

Dressed up to the nines - wearing your best clothes to go to a party or on a date - she was dressed up to the nines to go on her date.

All dolled up - wearing your best clothes to go to a party or on a date - she was dressed up to the nines to go on her date.

My neck of the woods - a place you know well - London is my neck of the woods, I grew up there.

The life and soul of the party - someone who brings life into an event - he was the life and soul of the party.

In the open air - outside - children like to play in the open air.

To be born with a silver spoon - to be born rich - he was born with a silver spoon.

To play your cards close to your chest - to keep something a secret - I tried to find out how much he would pay, but he played his cards close to his chest.

To let the cat out of the bag - to reveal a secret - it was supposed to be a surprise party, but someone let the cat out of the bag.

Pet peeve - something you dislike - I hate it when someone asks me that, it is one of my pet peeves.

To feel under the weather - to feel ill or down in spirit - I won't go to the party tonight because I feel under the weather.

All is not gold that glitters - everything that appears to be valuable is not valuable - Someone says, "I can't believe how cheap that diamond ring is." His friend answers, "All that glitters is not gold!"

As sound as a pound - reliable, dependable - my brother is as sound as a pound.

As the crow flies - in a straight line - I live three miles from you, as the crow flies.

Bite off more than you can chew - to take on too much - I took my IELTS test after just three weeks preparation and did not do well, I bit off more than I could chew.

Hit the hay - go to sleep - I was so tired after walking all day that I hit the hay as soon as I got home.

Honesty is the best policy - best to always be honest - I handed the wallet I found to the police, honesty is the best policy.

In the blink of an eye - estremely fast - That fighter plane was so fast, it passed in the blink of an eye.

As dry as a bone - very dry - That piece of cake I ate was as dry as a bone.

Not right in the head -crazy - She thinks she is related to the Queen, she is not right in the head.

Cut a long story short - in short - It was a long and complicated discussion, but to cut a long story short, we agreed to buy the car.

Under the weather - feeling unwell, but not clinically sick - I woke up this morning feeling a bit under the weather, but by the afternoon I was feeling fine.

Recharge his batteries - needs to take a rest and regain physical and mental strength - It has been a difficult month at work so I am going to Greece for a couple of weeks to recharge my batteries.

Looking like a death warmed up - someone slow and without energy - my son was out late last night and woke up this morning looking like a death warmed up.

On the road to recovery - recovering from illness - I have been ill with chicken pox, but I am now on the road to recovery.

As fit as a fiddle - active and agile - I am going to the disco this evening, I may be 65, but I feel as fit as a fiddle.

As right as rain - feeling well agin - I was ill last week, but I feel as right as rain now.

Off her trolley - crazy - She thinks she is related to the Queen, she is off her trolley.

A basket case - a person who has recently had a nervous breakdown - There is no hope for him, he is a basket case.

Popped his clogs - died a natural death - He popped his clogs yesterday. .

Bite the dust - died or was killed, usually doing something dangerous - The enemy soldiers attacked but I shot at them, they all bit the dust.

Kicked the bucket - died a natual death - he kicked the bucket on Friday.

A sore spot - if you say something to a man which causes offence, you have touched his sore spot - I told him that he had an ugly dog and he got angry, I must have touched a sore spot.

A taste of his own medicine - give back what a person gave to you - He didn't pay me for the work I did last year, so I refused to help him, he didn't like getting a piece of his own medicine.

A bitter pill to swallow - something that is difficult to accept - His wife left him for another man, even though he was a good husband, it was a bitter pill to swallow.

Have itchy feet - to want to travel - He cannot live in one place, he has itchy feet.

Jump for joy - be very happy - When she passed her exam, she jumped for joy.

Make my day - tempt someone to harm you so you can harm him twice as much - He told me he would punch me, I told him to make my day.

Out of sorts - feeling unwell - I am feeling out of sorts today.

A white lie - a harmless lie - I said I could not meet him because I had an appointment with the hairdresser, it was untrue, a white lie.

Misery guts - someone who complains a lot - He is always complaining, a right misery guts.

Puts a damper on - stops something from happening - We were going to go on a picnic, but it started raining so that put the damper on it.

Down in the dumps - feeling unenergetic - He is a bit down in the dumps today, he doesn't want to do anything.

Rub up the wrong way - annoy someone - He got angry when I told him that, I must have rubbed him up the wrong way.

Fed up to the teeth with - had enough of something, does not refer to food or eating - I am fed up with him always asking to borrow money.

Not on speaking terms - not talking to someone due to hostility - She is not on speaking terms with her ex husband.

Go spare - get angry - When I told him that, he went spare.

To take on board - to accept an idea or concept - He took on board the idea that he had been unfair to her.

Blow a fuse - lose one's temper - When he found out that she had had an affair, he blew a fuse.

Not the end of the world - not as serious as it seems - If I lose my job, it is not the end of the world.

To be economical with truth - to lie - I asked him if he had stolen the money, but he was economical with the truth.

Go off the deep end - get angry - When he found out that his son had taken his car, he went off the deep end.

Grin and bear it - withstand misfortune, hardship etc - My boss shouted at me today, but I just had to grin and bear it.

Had it up to here - said while gesturing to forehead, means had enough - My child has been naughty again, I have had it up to here.

Have a fit - in a semi-crazy panic - My washing machine broke down and flooded the kitchen, when I saw the mess, I had a fit.

Got a screw loose - mentally insane - If that is what he thinks then he must have a screw loose.

Drive someone up the wall - make someone crazy - That dog has been barking all day, he's driving me up the wall.

Keep your wits about you - be alert - That is a very dangerous road, keep your wits about you when you cross.

At my wits end - had enough of it, not food - That child of mine hasn't done his homework again, I don't know what to do, I'm at my wits end.

Put the cat among the pigeons - cause trouble by saying something - When I exposed them for lying, I really put the cat among the pigeons.

Drive someone around the bend - make someone crazy - That child's behaviour is driving me around the bend.

Keep out of the red - stay out of debt - I have a job, it doesn't pay much, but it keeps me out of the red.

Barely a shadow of - someone who is very ill - He has lost a lot of weight, he is barely a shadow of what he used to be.

Mind your p's and q's - be careful what you say - When you are dining with royalty, you should watch your p's and q's.

Give someone a earful - express a negative opinion to someone in a loud way - I was really angry and gave him a earful.

Get fed up with - enough - I am fed up with his complaining.

Gave him a piece of my mind - express a negative opinion in a firm, but measured way - I gave the shopkeeper a piece of my mind after he overcharged me.

Bite the hand that feeds you - do something that harms your source of income - I never cheat my employer, I don't believe in biting the hand that feeds you.

Kill the hen that lays the golden egg - do something that harms your source of income - I sold my business and now I am broke; I killed the hen that laid the golden egg.

Open a Pandora's box - start trouble - When the British had a referendum on Brexit, they opened a Pandora's box.

Have a sweet tooth - like sugary foods - My son has a sweet tooth.

Have green fingers - be good at gardening - My son has green fingers, he grows beautiful roses.

On the mend - improving in health - I have been ill but am on the mend now.

A dark horse - someone who says little and hides details about himself - He's a dark horse.
